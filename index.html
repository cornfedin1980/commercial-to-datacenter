01

The Systems Transfer No One Talks About

When people hear "commercial real estate" and "data center delivery" in the same sentence, they assume a wide gap. Different asset class. Different MEP complexity. Different risk profile. And honestly — they're not wrong about the asset class.

But they're wrong about what delivery actually requires.

At Canvas at Anna, I rebuilt the schedule from the critical path backward. I implemented daily structured standups across six subcontractor teams. I built a risk register tracking every open item, prioritized by severity and owner. I ran formal weekly reviews with the commissioning-adjacent stakeholders. I surfaced the $500K cost exposure in the amenity center before it became a change order crisis — through scope realignment and procurement restructuring, not scope reduction.

Every single one of those moves is how you run a data center program.

Governance isn't a product of the building type. It's a discipline you either have or you don't — and it transfers completely.

02

The Actual Overlap Is Larger Than You Think

I've spent the last several months immersed in data center delivery — studying the operators, the construction workflows, the commissioning frameworks. What I found surprised me: the structural DNA is nearly identical to high-complexity commercial construction. The vocabulary shifts. The stakes escalate. The tolerance for schedule variance drops to near zero. But the underlying mechanics?

Discipline Commercial RE Data Center
Schedule governance Critical path rebuilds, daily standups, trade sequencing → Same methodology. Tighter tolerances. More concurrent workstreams.
MEP coordination RFIs, submittals, pre-installation meetings, systems verification → Identical workflow. Higher consequence for sequencing failures.
Risk management Live registers, severity scoring, weekly executive reviews → Same tools. Risk register is your primary document, not a formality.
Commissioning prep Systems verification, punch management, stakeholder coordination → More structured, more credentialed — but same underlying discipline.
Budget governance Cost exposure identification, EVM, proactive variance reporting → Identical. Owners expect ≤5% variance. So do I.

The gap is real, not imaginary. High-voltage electrical systems at hyperscale density are different from commercial MEP — and I'm not pretending otherwise. That's exactly why I'm completing the Associate Commissioning Professional credential through BCxA and pursuing the NERC System Operator certification. The credentials are doing the technical bridging work. The governance systems already transferred.

03

What Mission-Critical Is Really Asking For

Here's what I've observed after dozens of conversations with data center owners, GCs, and developers: the pain is rarely technical. The pain is operational.

Projects fall apart because nobody owns the risk register. Nobody is running structured standups with accountability. Schedule slippage compounds because nobody rebuilt the critical path when the first delay hit. Commissioning gets compressed because the pre-Cx planning wasn't owned early enough. The MEP is fine — it's the delivery governance that failed.

That's a project management problem. And it's one I've been solving for ten-plus years.

The Pattern

Every program I've been brought into has the same fingerprint: good intentions, smart people, and a governance gap that nobody wanted to own. My job is to close that gap — fast, formally, and with documentation the owner can stand behind.

At Canvas at Anna, the recovery wasn't glamorous. It was disciplined. Daily standups. A risk register that actually got reviewed. A schedule rebuilt from constraints, not from optimism. Executive visibility into the right three metrics, not thirty. That recovery earned two follow-on project requests from the GC executive team — not because of the asset class, but because of the execution model.

04

The ACP credential and NERC SOC are in progress. The delivery discipline has been operational for a decade.
